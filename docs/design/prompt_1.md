# Story

Backgammon is one of the oldest known board games in human history, and its evolution spans thousands of years, moving through several ancient civilizations. It didn't emerge overnight in its current form; rather, it is the direct descendant of ancient Middle Eastern "race games."
Here is the lineage of where backgammon came from:

1. The Deepest Roots: Mesopotamia and Persia (The Cradle)
   The Royal Game of Ur (c. 2600 BCE): The earliest spiritual ancestor of backgammon was discovered in the 1920s in the Royal Tombs of Ur (modern-day Iraq). It used a board of 20 squares, pyramidal dice, and checkers that raced each other along a set path.
   Nard / Shahr-e Sukhteh (c. 3000–2500 BCE): In southeastern Iran, archeologists discovered a board game at the "Burnt City" (Shahr-e Sukhteh) featuring 60 markers made of agate and turquoise, alongside ebony dice. This evolved directly into the Persian game of Nard (or Takhteh Nard), which features a board structure, 30 checkers, and rules that are nearly identical to modern backgammon.
2. The Roman Evolution: Ludus Duodecim Scriptorum and Tabula
   The Romans took the Middle Eastern concepts and popularized them across Europe:
   They began with Ludus Duodecim Scriptorum ("Game of Twelve Lines"), which used three rows of 12 points.
   By the 1st century CE, this was streamlined into Tabula ("Table"), which dropped one row of points. Tabula used 24 points, 30 checkers, and dice, making it the first game virtually indistinguishable from backgammon. The Emperor Claudius was famously so obsessed with Tabula that he had a board built into his chariot so he could play on bumpy roads.
3. The East Asian Branches
   As trade routes flourished, the Persian Nard traveled eastward along the Silk Road:
   It entered China during the Western Wei dynasty (6th century CE) as Shuanglu ("Double Sixes"), becoming highly popular among the elite during the Tang and Song dynasties.
   From China, it traveled to Japan, where it was known as Sugoroku (specifically Ban-Sugoroku, the board version), though it was eventually banned several times by authorities due to its heavy association with high-stakes gambling.
4. The English Naming (17th Century)
   The game arrived in England via the Crusades (where it had been called "Tables"). In the early 1600s, the English modified the rules slightly—allowing legal doubles and changing the starting positions—and rebranded it as Backgammon.
   What does the name mean? > The most widely accepted origin comes from Middle English: "Back" (because captured pieces are sent back to the start) + "Gamen" (the Old English word for game).
5. The 1920s Modernization
   While the board and movement are thousands of years old, the modern, highly competitive game you play today was finalized in New York City. In the 1920s, an unknown gambler at a lower Manhattan gaming club introduced the Doubling Cube. This single addition turned a casual game of luck and racing into a high-stakes psychological sport of probability and financial strategy.

6. The Analytical Revolution (gnubg & The Neural Net Era)
   In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, backgammon entered the digital age. The pivotal milestone for the Western world was the creation of gnubg (GNU Backgammon), a powerful, open-source neural network evaluation engine. Alongside commercial pioneers like Jellyfish and Snowie, gnubg completely dismantled centuries of human dogma. By playing millions of games against itself, the AI proved that traditional strategies were often too conservative. It taught players to value flexibility, take calculated risks early, and view the board through pure, cold probability. Today, no human can beat a top-tier bot, and the world’s best players train strictly against neural nets to perfect their mathematical precision.

3. The Imperial Courtyard (The Shuanglu & Sugoroku Era)
   Between the 6th and 14th centuries, the game migrated along the Silk Road into China and Japan. In China, it became Shuanglu ("Double Sixes"), evolving into a deeply strategic courtly obsession favored by emperors, poets, and high officials during the Tang and Song dynasties. When it crossed the sea to Japan as Ban-Sugoroku, it shed its aristocratic exclusivity and exploded into the public sphere. It became so fiercely competitive and deeply tied to high-stakes street gambling that the Japanese shogunate officially banned it multiple times over the centuries. This era represents the game at its peak of cultural prestige on one hand, and raw, high-stakes adrenaline on the other.
