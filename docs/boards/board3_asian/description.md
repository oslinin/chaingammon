# Board 3 — East Asian (The Shuanglu & Sugoroku Era, 6th–14th century CE)

## Historical Context

Persian Nard traveled east along the Silk Road during the Western Wei dynasty (6th century CE), arriving in China as **Shuanglu** ("Double Sixes"). During the Tang and Song dynasties it became a courtly obsession among emperors, poets, and high officials. Crossing the sea to Japan as **Ban-Sugoroku**, it shed its aristocratic exclusivity and exploded into the public sphere — becoming so tied to high-stakes street gambling that the shogunate officially banned it multiple times. This era represents the game at its peak of cultural prestige and raw adrenaline simultaneously.

**Board aesthetic:** Glossy black lacquer, hand-painted gold leaf, green jade, and white river stone. Deep black and gold with mother-of-pearl point inlays and plum blossom border details.

## Character: Lady Meiling

A brilliant Tang Dynasty noblewoman and court scholar, seated in an open-air courtyard surrounded by blossoming plum trees. She wears an exquisite embroidered silk hanfu and places carved jade checkers with total composure.

**Playing style — The Hidden Dagger.** Meiling's style is absolute emotional detachment and misdirection. She specializes in deep back-game strategies — intentionally letting pieces get captured early to hold anchor points deep in enemy territory. Just when the opponent thinks they are cruising to victory, she springs a devastating trap, turning the entire game in a single turn.

## Coin

The coin is a Tang Dynasty **Kaiyuan Tongbao** cash coin — a round bronze disk with a square hole at its center, the iconic currency of the Silk Road, rendered in aged bronze with gold inlay calligraphy.
