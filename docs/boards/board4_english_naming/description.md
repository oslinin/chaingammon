# Board 4 — English Naming (The Restoration Era, 1640s–1660s CE)

## Historical Context

The game arrived in England via the Crusades, where it had long been called **Tables**. In the early 1640s–1660s, Restoration-era England modified the rules — legalizing doubles and changing starting positions — and rebranded it **Backgammon**. The name fuses Middle English: *Back* (captured pieces sent back to start) + *Gamen* (Old English for "game"). From smoky London taverns to the country houses of the gentry, backgammon became the game of Restoration England.

**Board aesthetic:** Rugged dark-oak tavern aesthetic — candlelight, pewter tankards, dark polished wood, and Tudor heraldry. Deep walnut tones with iron-nail border studs and red-and-white Tudor rose accents.

## Character: Lord Charles Cavendish

A composed English nobleman of the Restoration court, wearing dark wool and a lace cravat, seated at a heavy oak table in a candlelit London club. He plays deliberately, a glass of port nearby, and keeps a leather-bound notebook of odds calculations within reach.

**Playing style — The Rules Reformer.** Cavendish plays the new English game with a precise, legalistic mind. He exploits the newly legal doubling of doubles and the revised starting positions to run an efficient, structured race — respecting probability tables over instinct, and pressing every technical advantage the new rules allow.
