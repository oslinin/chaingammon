# Board 1 — Persian (The Nard Era, c. 3000–600 BCE)

## Historical Context

The oldest confirmed ancestor of backgammon was unearthed at the "Burnt City" (Shahr-e Sukhteh) in southeastern Iran — a board with 60 markers of agate and turquoise and ebony dice, dating to roughly 2500 BCE. This evolved into **Nard** (Takhteh Nard), the Persian board game featuring 24 points, 30 checkers, and rules nearly identical to modern backgammon. Nard was the game of grand viziers, silk merchants, and court scholars — a meditation on fate, patience, and the long game.

**Board aesthetic:** Old-world luxury — desert sand, raw silk, polished agate, and turquoise. Rich amber and deep emerald tones with ornate geometric border patterns drawn from Persian tilework.

## Character: Bozorgmehr the Wise

A dignified, gray-bearded Grand Vizier seated on an intricate silk rug in a sunlit pavilion. He wears a flowing robe of deep emerald and moves heavy, smooth turquoise checkers with deliberate grace.

**Playing style — The Patient Weaver.** Bozorgmehr reads the board as a grand tapestry of destiny. He rarely dashes; instead he builds a formidable home board, seals escape routes, and lets a superior defensive structure do the work. Patience is his weapon, and time always rewards it.

## Coin

The coin depicts a turquoise-and-agate medallion — the very materials used in the Shahr-e Sukhteh game pieces — set in polished gold, evoking the treasure rooms of ancient Persia.
