# Chaingammon — Mission Statement

## The Problem

Your backgammon rating is not yours.

When you spend years climbing the ladder on Backgammon King, Play65, or any other platform, that rating lives in their database. It is locked behind their login wall, subject to their rules, and gone if they shut down. Switch platforms and you start at zero — a stranger, again, with nothing to show for your history.

Skill should be portable. Reputation should be owned by the person who earned it.

## The Solution

Chaingammon puts ELO ratings on-chain.

Every match result, every rating update, every point gained or lost is recorded on a public blockchain — immutable, transparent, and owned by no one except the players themselves. Your rating is stored in your wallet, not in a company's database.

## The Vision

A permissionless backgammon ecosystem where your rating follows you everywhere.

Any front-end — whether it's Chaingammon's own interface, a third-party client, or an interface built years from now — can read from and write to the same on-chain rating registry. Competition history becomes a public good, like DNS or email: an open protocol that no single entity controls.

## Core Principles

**Player-owned identity.** Your wallet is your player profile. No registration, no username/password, no platform lock-in.

**Transparent ELO.** Rating calculations are executed on-chain or verifiably off-chain. Anyone can audit them. No secret matchmaking formulas, no hidden adjustments.

**Open protocol.** Chaingammon is a standard, not a product. The smart contracts are public. Build your own client, your own tournament system, your own league — all backed by the same shared rating graph.

**Portable reputation.** A 1600-rated player on Chaingammon is a 1600-rated player everywhere that speaks the protocol. Ratings are credentials you carry, not tokens you borrow.

## Why Web3?

This is an obvious use case for blockchain technology: persistent, shared state that no single party should own. Backgammon ratings are a perfect fit — they are high-stakes enough to matter, simple enough to compute on-chain, and have been trapped in silos for decades waiting for a better model.

Chaingammon is that model.
